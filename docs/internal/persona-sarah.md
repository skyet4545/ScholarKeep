# Sarah — the persona to run every decision through

_Reusable persona for evaluating ScholarKeep changes. When in doubt, ask: "What would Sarah think?"_

## Who she is

**Sarah Whitlock.** 38. Lives in Lutz, FL (Tampa Bay suburb, Hillsborough County). Married 14 years to David, who's a project manager. Three kids:
- **Maya, 11** — autism diagnosis at age 5, on **FES-UA**. Receives ABA therapy 2x/week, weekly OT, vision therapy. Pulled from public school in 3rd grade after the IEP fell apart.
- **Ezra, 8** — neurotypical. On **PEP**. Loves Lego, hates worksheets.
- **June, 5** — kindergarten this year. On **PEP**. Just starting.

Total household scholarship: ~$25K across the three. Single income; David's salary covers the mortgage and basics, Sarah's freelance graphic design covers extras. They are tight on money.

Sarah was a public school teacher before kids. Switched to part-time freelance to homeschool. She is **deeply competent** — she reads the Purchasing Guides front to back twice a year — and **deeply tired**.

## Her current tools for ESA admin

- **Manila folder** of physical receipts. She still mostly does paper.
- **Notes.app** with a list "submit before end of month"
- **EMA portal** that she hates and avoids
- **Cozi Gold** ($39/yr) for family calendar — uses daily, trusts it
- **Erin Condren** physical planner ($60) — yearly purchase, tracks homeschool log
- **Facebook**: in 7 FL homeschool groups, lurks more than posts
- **Instagram**: follows ~12 homeschool moms, mostly for curriculum ideas

She **has not** tried HeyPeppy — she's heard the name in FB groups but the $12/mo made her bounce. "I'm not paying $144 a year for an app."

## What she believes

- **Companies will betray you eventually.** Cozi raised prices twice. Evernote went insane. She's wary of subscriptions in general.
- **"AI" usually means "we sold your data."** She's skeptical, not hostile. If AI is genuinely useful AND doesn't require giving her data away, she'll use it. But the burden of proof is on the tool.
- **Trust comes from other moms.** Not from companies, not from ads. A recommendation from Amanda in her co-op carries more weight than 100 5-star reviews.
- **Time is money in a way only moms understand.** A tool that saves her 30 minutes a week is genuinely valuable. A tool that costs her 30 minutes to set up is a non-starter.
- **She's not political at the table.** She voted for school choice but she doesn't want to talk about it. She wants quiet competence, not flag-waving.
- **She doesn't want to look stupid.** If she pulls out her phone in front of her co-op and the app is clunky or confusing, she'll never open it again.

## What burns her

1. **Hidden subscription costs.** Trial that auto-converts. Pricing on page 3.
2. **Tech-bro voice.** "Revolutionize," "transform," "AI-powered," "synergy."
3. **Apps designed for someone other than moms.** Black backgrounds, neon, "rise and grind" energy. She's not that.
4. **Anything that smells political.** Left or right. Even subtle.
5. **Onboarding that asks for too much before showing value.** If the first screen is "create an account," she's out.
6. **"Beta" being an excuse.** If something is broken, she'll forgive it once. Twice, she's gone.

## What earns her trust instantly

1. **Pricing on the homepage, in the hero.** Not behind a click.
2. **A real person's name and face.** Not "the team" or stock photos.
3. **Specific Florida vocabulary.** "FES-UA," "Step Up," "EMA portal" — used correctly.
4. **An explicit "we don't share your data with Step Up / FLDOE" line.**
5. **A free tier that's actually useful.** Not crippled.
6. **A clear "this is what's free, this is what's paid" table.**
7. **An obvious way to delete everything.** It signals confidence.
8. **A founder who talks like a parent, not a startup CEO.**

## How she discovers new things

1. Mention in a FB group thread she's already reading
2. A specific mom she trusts says "we use X"
3. FPEA convention vendor booth (if she goes)
4. Podcast sponsor read on a show she already listens to
5. Almost never: App Store organic search. Almost never: paid ads.

## What she sounds like

- "Y'all" sometimes, "you guys" more often
- Says "my homeschool" not "our school"
- Says "my kids," not "the children"
- Says "the scholarship" or "Step Up" — never "ESA program"
- Will say "praying for you" in DMs to other moms — but won't expect it from a brand
- Tone register: warm, busy, brief, kind

## Her single biggest unanswered question

> "Is this actually built for me, or for some idea of me by someone who's never done this?"

Every word on the site, every screen in the app, every decision the founders make either answers that question yes or no.

---

## How to use this persona

When making any product decision — copy, feature, design, pricing — ask:
1. **Would Sarah understand this on first read at 9:30pm?**
2. **Does this earn her trust or burn it?**
3. **Does this respect her time?**
4. **Is the voice right (mom-warm, not tech-bro)?**
5. **Would she be embarrassed to use this in front of her co-op?**

If any of these answers is "no," fix it before shipping.
